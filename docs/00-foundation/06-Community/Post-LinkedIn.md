## English Version

**Post text** (LinkedIn optimal: ~300 words with line breaks for readability):

---

I'm 55. I haven't written code in 30 years.

The last time was 1994 — Assembler and Borland C++ for my graduation thesis: a graphics application for designing electronic circuit boards. Object-oriented programming deeply shaped how I think. Then I moved into tech management and never looked back.

My career was CMCSISG, VNG Cloud, Galaxy Holdings. Boardrooms, not IDEs.

In May 2025, I had a problem.

My development team at MTS was slow to adopt AI. They feared AI-generated code was full of bugs — "more time fixing than coding." They used ChatGPT and Gemini individually for quick prompts, but had no team-wide process.

I brought in experts. Ran a Claude Code workshop. But the team was still resistant.

And honestly? I didn't fully understand what they were doing either. Or exactly how AI tools would help. My team was polite but unconvinced — the boss isn't a real software engineer.

**But maybe that was my advantage.** No legacy habits. No "this is how we've always done it." Everything was new — so I was ready to learn everything new.

I saw other teams, other companies succeeding. So I made a decision that surprised everyone:

I would learn it myself.

I started with Python. Then free tools — LM Studio, Ollama with Continue.dev. Then paid — GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Claude Code. Small apps first. Then real enterprise platforms — Bflow (bflow.vn — ERP+BPM Platform for Vietnamese SMEs, built by my MTS team, launched Oct 2024), then evolving it into Bflow 2.0 (ERP+BPM+AI — Conversation-First with AI as a core pillar), and NQH-Bot (AI-Powered Workforce Management for Vietnamese F&B market, at Nhat Quang Holding — my second startup).

That's when things got real. And messy.

NQH-Bot — auto-scheduling, multi-tenant SaaS, regional compliance: 679 mock implementations. 78% production failure. The AI was fast, but ungoverned.

I didn't blame the tools. I built a framework.

Over 8 months, that framework went through 12 major versions — tested across 5 real projects. Every failure became a pattern. Every pattern became a rule. The idea for governance emerged from the pain of projects growing beyond control.

During Lunar New Year — between family ceremonies and welcoming guests — I wrote TinySDLC. At 55. Using the very AI tools I was learning to govern.

Today I'm sharing two projects:

**TinySDLC** — agent orchestrator with SDLC role discipline for AI coding (MIT)
8 roles, separation of duties, security hardening, multi-channel. Zero infrastructure.
https://github.com/Minh-Tam-Solution/tinysdlc

**MTS-SDLC-Lite** — the governance methodology playbook (MIT)
https://github.com/Minh-Tam-Solution/MTS-SDLC-Lite

Here's what I've realized: we are always programming in our lives. With AI today, anyone with design thinking, systems thinking, and domain knowledge can quickly experiment and turn ideas into products.

AI is not the competitive advantage.
**Discipline is.**

This is not about me learning to code.
This is about redesigning how teams work with AI.

If your team is using AI coding tools without structure or role discipline — these are for you.
